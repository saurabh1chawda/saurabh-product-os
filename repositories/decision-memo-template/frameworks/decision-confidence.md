# Decision Confidence

Status: v1 framework  
Last updated: July 2026

## Purpose

Decision confidence communicates how strongly a recommendation is supported by evidence and how much risk remains.

Confidence should influence the recommendation, the rollout path, and the review cadence.

## Confidence Levels

| Confidence | Meaning | Recommended Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Low | Evidence is weak, assumptions are untested, or risk is poorly understood | Validate further, prototype, or defer |
| Medium | Evidence supports the direction, but important assumptions remain | Proceed with constraints, milestones, and review triggers |
| High | Evidence is strong, trade-offs are understood, and risks have owners | Approve, execute, and measure outcomes |

## Low Confidence

Use when:

- The customer problem is unclear.
- Evidence is mostly opinion.
- Alternatives have not been compared.
- Risks are material and unresolved.
- Success metrics are vague.

Memo implication: do not ask for full-scale approval. Ask for validation, discovery, or a limited test.

## Medium Confidence

Use when:

- The problem is clear.
- Evidence supports the recommendation.
- Some assumptions remain.
- The decision is reversible or can be staged.
- Metrics and review points are defined.

Memo implication: recommend a constrained approval with explicit review triggers.

## High Confidence

Use when:

- Customer and business evidence are strong.
- Alternatives were considered seriously.
- Trade-offs are explicit.
- Risks have owners and mitigations.
- Success criteria are measurable.

Memo implication: ask for approval and execution alignment.

## Writing Guidance

Do not inflate confidence to win approval.

Strong decision memos earn trust by matching the recommendation to the evidence quality. Low confidence is not failure; it is a signal to narrow scope, validate faster, or avoid premature commitment.
