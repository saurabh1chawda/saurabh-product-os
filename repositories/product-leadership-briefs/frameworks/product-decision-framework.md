# Product Decision Framework

Status: v1 reusable framework  
Last updated: July 2026

The Product Decision Framework is the standard structure behind every Product Leadership Brief. It is designed to show product judgment clearly, not to create a long case-study narrative.

## 1. Context

Describe the business, customer, product, and organizational setting.

Good context explains why the decision mattered before explaining what was built.

## 2. Problem

Define the product problem in a way that separates symptoms from the underlying constraint.

The problem should be specific enough that different strategic options can be evaluated against it.

## 3. Options Considered

List the credible paths available at the time.

Each option should include:

- What it would prioritize
- Why it was attractive
- What risks it carried
- Why it was chosen or rejected

## 4. Decision

State the decision plainly.

The decision should be memorable, specific, and connected to the highest-leverage constraint.

## 5. Trade-offs

Explain what the team accepted, delayed, protected, or gave up.

A strong brief does not pretend that the decision was obvious. It shows the cost of choosing well.

## 6. Stakeholder Alignment

Describe the cross-functional disagreement or tension.

Strong product leadership often means translating competing functional priorities into one shared definition of success.

## 7. Results

Report outcomes in business, customer, product, platform, or operating terms.

Results should be concise and tied to the original decision.

## 8. Lessons Learned

Explain what changed in the product leader's thinking because of the work.

This section should include reflection, not just validation.

## 9. Reusable Principle

Extract the product leadership principle that can apply beyond the original company or project.

Examples:

- Adoption before expansion.
- Platform before intelligence.
- Optimization before scale.
- Trust before innovation.

## Quality Checklist

- The decision is visible within the first minute.
- The problem is specific and meaningful.
- Options show real strategic alternatives.
- Trade-offs are explicit.
- Stakeholder tensions are treated fairly.
- Results connect to the decision.
- The principle is reusable beyond the brief.
