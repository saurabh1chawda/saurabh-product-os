# Decision Reusability

Status: v1 framework  
Last updated: July 2026

## Purpose

Decision Reusability helps Product Managers extract useful lessons from historical product decisions without copying context blindly.

Not every lesson transfers equally. A good Product Leadership Brief should separate what was specific to the situation from what can guide future decisions.

## Reusability Levels

| Level | Meaning | How To Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Context-Specific | The lesson depends heavily on company, market, customer, or technical context | Use as a caution or example, not a rule |
| Adaptable | The decision pattern can transfer if constraints are similar | Adjust to the new customer, evidence, and risk profile |
| Universal | The principle applies across many product contexts | Use as a leadership heuristic |

## Context-Specific

Use when:

- The decision depended on unique data, regulation, business model, or technical architecture.
- The outcome would not generalize without similar constraints.
- The lesson is useful mainly as a historical example.

Example: a migration sequence shaped by one company's legacy architecture.

## Adaptable

Use when:

- The decision pattern applies to similar product situations.
- The exact execution must change by domain.
- The trade-off pattern is more reusable than the implementation.

Example: optimizing a funnel before increasing acquisition spend.

## Universal

Use when:

- The principle helps across domains.
- The decision logic applies to AI, SaaS, growth, platform, or payments contexts.
- The lesson improves judgment even when the implementation differs.

Example: measure outcomes, not output.

## Extraction Questions

- What was unique to the original context?
- What decision pattern repeated across other contexts?
- What trade-off would another PM likely face?
- What evidence would be needed before applying this elsewhere?
- Is the lesson context-specific, adaptable, or universal?

## Writing Guidance

Do not overgeneralize success.

The best briefs teach judgment by showing the boundary between what worked once and what can be reused.
