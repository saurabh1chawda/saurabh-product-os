# Anti-Patterns

Status: v1 signature framework component  
Last updated: July 2026

Anti-patterns identify situations where AI should not be prioritized, even when the idea sounds impressive.

## 1. Rules Outperform AI

AI should not be prioritized when deterministic rules, workflow design, search, filtering, or simple automation solve the customer problem more reliably.

Decision: Use simpler software first.

## 2. No Customer Value

AI should not be prioritized when the primary reason is novelty, competitive pressure, or executive enthusiasm rather than a meaningful customer workflow improvement.

Decision: Reject or reframe the opportunity.

## 3. Human Approval Is Mandatory

AI should not be prioritized as automation when the workflow requires human judgment, accountability, or approval at every meaningful step.

Decision: Consider assistive AI or copilot support instead of automation.

## 4. High Cost, Low Impact

AI should not be prioritized when inference, infrastructure, data, operational, or support costs are not proportional to customer or business value.

Decision: Park or validate economics before investment.

## 5. Low Organizational Readiness

AI should not be prioritized when the team lacks data quality, evaluation discipline, operational support, monitoring, or clear ownership.

Decision: Invest in readiness before productization.

## 6. Trust Risk Is Unmanaged

AI should not be prioritized when failures are high-impact and the product lacks explainability, override, fallback, escalation, or auditability.

Decision: Reduce automation depth or add safeguards first.

## 7. Success Cannot Be Measured

AI should not be prioritized when teams cannot define which customer behavior, workflow outcome, or business metric should improve.

Decision: Validate measurement before roadmap commitment.

## 8. Demo Value Replaces Workflow Value

AI should not be prioritized when the product looks impressive in a demo but does not fit the user's actual workflow.

Decision: Run workflow discovery before investment.
