# Evidence Ladder

Status: v1 signature framework  
Last updated: July 2026

## Purpose

The Evidence Ladder helps teams understand the quality of discovery evidence.

Not all evidence is equal. A confident stakeholder opinion is not the same as repeated customer behavior. The ladder helps teams move from weak signals to stronger validation before committing product investment.

## The Ladder

```text
Validated Behavior
        ^
Experiment Results
        ^
Prototype Interaction
        ^
Observed Workflow
        ^
Repeated Customer Pattern
        ^
Customer Interview Signal
        ^
Stakeholder Opinion
        ^
Internal Assumption
```

## Levels

### 1. Internal Assumption

What the team believes without direct evidence.

Use for: Starting discovery.

Do not use for: Roadmap commitment.

### 2. Stakeholder Opinion

What internal teams believe based on customer contact, business pressure, or operational experience.

Use for: Signal gathering.

Do not use for: Final validation.

### 3. Customer Interview Signal

What customers say about their problems, workflows, constraints, and desired outcomes.

Use for: Understanding language and pain.

Watch for: Polite feedback, hypotheticals, and solution bias.

### 4. Repeated Customer Pattern

The same pain, workflow, or workaround appears across multiple customers or segments.

Use for: Problem confidence.

Watch for: Segment differences.

### 5. Observed Workflow

The team sees the customer behavior directly through shadowing, recordings, support sessions, analytics, or workflow walkthroughs.

Use for: Separating stated needs from real behavior.

### 6. Prototype Interaction

Customers interact with a prototype, concept, or workflow simulation.

Use for: Testing comprehension, usability, desirability, and trust.

### 7. Experiment Results

A structured test produces measurable evidence against a specific assumption.

Use for: Investment decisions.

### 8. Validated Behavior

Customers repeatedly use, adopt, pay for, switch to, or depend on the solution.

Use for: Scaling decisions.

## Decision Rule

The more expensive the product decision, the higher the evidence should be on the ladder.

Low-cost exploration can start with assumptions and interviews. High-cost roadmap, engineering, or GTM commitments should require observed behavior, prototype interaction, experiment results, or validated behavior.
